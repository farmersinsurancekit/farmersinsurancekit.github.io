Public Adjuster Tips for Choosing a Roofing Contractor After Damage

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog Details

The sky has a new color in many parts of the world. It’s not the serene blue of a postcard, but the ominous, swirling gray of a supercell, or the oppressive, smoke-laden orange of a distant wildfire. In an era defined by escalating climate volatility, a damaged roof is no longer a random act of misfortune; it is, for many, an inevitable encounter with a new global reality. Your roof, that silent sentinel against the elements, has just failed its most critical test. In the disorienting aftermath, amidst the stress and the urgency, your next decision—choosing the right roofing contractor—will determine whether you embark on a path of secure recovery or descend into a nightmare of compounding losses.

As a public adjuster, I have stood with countless homeowners at this exact crossroads. I have seen the best and worst of the restoration industry. I have witnessed contractors who are true artisans and community pillars, and I have battled others who are little more than opportunistic predators in a truck. The difference between these outcomes often hinges on the homeowner's knowledge before the first contract is signed. This is not just about fixing shingles; it’s about protecting your most significant financial asset and ensuring your family’s safety in an increasingly unpredictable world.

The New Reality: Why Your Choice Matters More Than Ever

The landscape of property damage is shifting beneath our feet. The "once-in-a-century" storm now seems to visit every few years. Supply chain disruptions, a lingering global issue, can make materials scarce and expensive. Labor shortages mean skilled roofers are in high demand. This perfect storm of factors has created a fertile ground for what the industry calls "storm chasers."

The Storm Chaser Phenomenon

These are nomadic contractors who flood a region after a major weather event. They often have persuasive sales tactics, offering "free" inspections and promising to handle everything with your insurance company. The danger is their transient nature. Once the work in your area dries up, they pack up and move to the next disaster zone. If your roof fails in six months or a year, they are long gone, and any warranty they provided is worthless. Your choice must be a contractor with deep, verifiable roots in your community, someone who will be there to answer the phone when the next hailstorm hits.

The Insurance Tightrope

Your relationship with your insurance company is a delicate dance. They have a fiduciary duty to their shareholders, not to you. The settlement they initially offer is often a starting point, not a final figure. A quality roofing contractor understands this dynamic and can provide detailed, defensible estimates that justify the true cost of repair. A subpar contractor might lowball the estimate to get the job quickly, leading to corner-cutting, or they might engage in fraudulent practices that can jeopardize your entire insurance claim. Your contractor should be your ally in this process, not a liability.

The Public Adjuster's Blueprint for Vetting a Roofer

Forget the flashy truck wraps and the slick website. The following criteria are what we, as public adjusters, use to separate the professionals from the predators.

1. Scrutinize Credentials and Local Presence

This is the non-negotiable first step.

  • Licensing and Insurance: Do not accept a verbal assurance. Ask for their state-issued contractor’s license number and their certificate of insurance for both liability and workers' compensation. Verify the license is active and in good standing with your state’s licensing board. Call the insurance agent listed on the certificate to confirm the policies are current. If a worker falls off your roof and the contractor lacks proper insurance, you, the homeowner, could be held liable for medical bills and lost wages.
  • Physical Address: A P.O. Box is a red flag. You want a company with a physical, commercial office space in your state or region. Drive by it. A established business is invested in its reputation and is less likely to disappear.
  • Manufacturer Certifications: Top shingle manufacturers like GAF, Owens Corning, and CertainTeed have rigorous certification programs for contractors. To become a "Master Elite" or "Preferred" contractor, the roofer must demonstrate proper training, insurance, and a proven track record. This is a powerful third-party endorsement of their quality and professionalism.

2. Demand a Robust Portfolio and Verifiable References

Anyone can show you a few glossy photos.

  • Project Portfolio: Ask to see a portfolio of completed projects, specifically ones that involved insurance claims for damage similar to yours (hail, wind, etc.). Look for consistency and quality in the workmanship.
  • Recent References: Politely insist on contact information for at least three clients from the last 6-12 months. Call them. Ask specific questions: Was the project completed on time and on budget? Was the site kept clean? How was the communication? Did they encounter any unexpected issues, and if so, how were they resolved? Would they hire this contractor again?

3. Decipher the Estimate and Contract

The devil is truly in the details here. A vague proposal is a recipe for disaster.

  • Line-Item Specificity: The estimate should be incredibly detailed. It should not just say "Replace roof." It must list specific materials by brand, style, and color. It should detail the scope of work: "Remove and dispose of existing shingles and underlayment," "Install ice and water shield in valleys and eaves," "Replace all pipe jacks and vent flashings," "Install new synthetic underlayment," etc.
  • Code Compliance: This is critical. Building codes are constantly updated, especially regarding roofing in high-wind zones. The contract should explicitly state that all work will be performed in compliance with local and international building codes. Failure to do so can lead to failed inspections and voided insurance coverage down the line.
  • Warranties: Understand the Layers: There are typically two warranties:
    • Manufacturer's Warranty: Covers defects in the shingles themselves. This is important.
    • Contractor's Workmanship Warranty: This is the most important one. It covers errors in installation. How long is it? A reputable contractor will offer a warranty of 5, 10, or even 25 years on their labor. Get it in writing. A one-year warranty is often insufficient.

4. Navigate the Insurance Payment Process with Clarity

This is where many homeowners get confused and vulnerable.

  • Understand the Two-Party System: Your insurance company will typically issue checks in two disbursements. The first is for the Actual Cash Value (ACV), which is the depreciated value of your roof. The second is for the Recoverable Depreciation, held back until the work is complete.
  • Beware of the "Assignment of Benefits" (AOB): Some contractors will pressure you to sign an AOB. This is a legal document that assigns your insurance benefits directly to them. While it can streamline payments, it also signs away your control over the insurance funds. You lose your leverage. If the contractor does a bad job or disputes the scope with the insurer, you are stuck in the middle, but the money is already in their hands. I generally advise clients to avoid AOBs. It is safer for you to receive the payment from your insurer and pay the contractor directly, maintaining control throughout the process.
  • Communication is Key: Your chosen contractor should be willing to communicate directly with your insurance adjuster to clarify the scope of work and ensure the estimate aligns with the insurance settlement. However, they should not negotiate on your behalf for a higher settlement—that is the role of a public adjuster.

Red Flags That Should Send You Running

Your gut instinct is a powerful tool. If something feels off, it probably is.

  • High-Pressure Sales Tactics: "This price is only good today," or "We just have extra materials from another job." This is a classic sign of a scam.
  • Requiring Full Payment Upfront: A reasonable deposit for materials is standard (e.g., 10-30%). Demanding full payment before a single shingle is delivered is a major red flag.
  • Unsolicited Door-Knocking: Legitimate, busy contractors do not need to go door-to-door to find work after a storm.
  • Cash-Only Deals: This is often a tactic to avoid taxes and insurance obligations, and it offers you no financial recourse.
  • Vague or Verbal-Only Agreements: Everything must be in writing. Every promise, every specification, every warranty.

In this new age of climate disruption, a damaged roof is a call to action. It demands a response that is informed, deliberate, and strategic. By adopting the mindset of a public adjuster—by prioritizing verification over persuasion, and substance over salesmanship—you can transform a potential crisis into a managed, successful recovery. Your home is your shelter, your sanctuary, and your investment. Do not entrust its repair to anyone who cannot pass the most rigorous test of their credentials, their reputation, and their commitment to your long-term security. The right contractor is not just a vendor; they are a partner in restoring your peace of mind.
